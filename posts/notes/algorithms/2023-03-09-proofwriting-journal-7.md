This week, we learned about a few different proof techniques (namely: direct, contrapositive, and by contradiction) and read about some good mathematical writing practices. 

Which of our proof techniques do you feel the most comfortable using and why? How was understanding these proof structure affected the way you approach problem-solving, if at all?

Looking at section 5.3 on good mathematical writing practices, which of the rules do you think you are most guilty of breaking in prior mathematical work? Do you understand/agree with the guidance given in this chapter, or were there suggestions that didn't feel natural to you?


I feel the most comfortable working with direct proof and with proof by contradiction, which are typically the first strategies I try. I really like the contrapositive but I'm not very good at recognizing cases in which it might apply. Understanding these proof structures have changed the way that I look at problems. For example, I don't think I've ever used "problem statement" in a proof until this class. This reflects the shift in my thinking about problems from a very ambiguous "try different configurations until you find something that works" to "understand what it would take to prove the statement and work from both ends".

Most of the guidance makes sense except for (2). I definitely don't end mathematical statements with a period, on an aesthetic basis. I'm not sure if I see this very commonly in the wild either, because that would leave "hanging periods" after centered equations. 

I'm guilty of not separating mathematical statements and words very well; a lot of times, I'll jump ahead with the math and write very terse English to accommpany it. I do use the active voice, and I think my use of symbols is just enough to work. I really enjoy elegance and will try to restructure my proofs to follow naturally. I think I usually have statements following joining words, but sometimes if it's simplifying a single term I'm not very careful.

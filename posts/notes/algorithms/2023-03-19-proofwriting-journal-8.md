Recently, we've made space for analyzing some of our longer proofs. Last Friday, we broke up the Proposition on page 153 into smaller claims and this Friday (unless you're doing this really) we used Toulmin Analysis, in addition to our proof techniques, to analyze two proofs related to the characterization of even perfect numbers. 

Reflect on these experiences:

    what aspects of the activities did you find useful?
    which did you struggle with?
    do you imagine yourself using these techniques when you encounter challenging proofs in your other math courses? 


I'm mostly neutral on the usefulness of Toulmin Analysis as a whole. On one hand, the structure it provides doesn't seem to do much; a proof is already inherently a series of statements and justifications, and the distinction between the warrant and backing is not very obvious. After all, if a warrant requires enough backing then it should be part of the proof already! So this kind of deconstruction doesn't seem to do much.

On the other hand, when I read through a proof I think that my mental process does shift between three states, not two. It's not just between claims and warrants, but also testing each warrant in my head and feeling if they have sufficient justification. One thing that does help is going through my lack of understanding out loud (the rubber ducky approach). 

I agree that later on math courses will involve proofs that you can't justify on an intuitive level, and that this kind of incremental reduction can be really useful then. I think I need to work on being explicit with which step of the process I am on, and communicating my thoughts when I have them. A problem I used to think I only had in other classes (but now I think also applies in math) is having a subtly incorrect mental model of core concepts, and as a result not being able to progress because of that. It can feel really frustrating when you look at the solution and feel that the answer was super reachable -- in those cases I think I should acknowledge my lack of understanding and slow down in order to get those concepts fully.

PS: I attended SUMS this weekend and found the talk on mathematical education really interesting. There was the notion of the "didactic contract", where there might be a problem like "A farmer has 10 sheep and 26 goats. How old is he?", and a lot about how problem-solving developed in humans and as a species. I think one of the single most important things I've learned in math this year is a kind of finicky truth: the proof often mirrors the problem. The proof supervenes on the problem, the reason for something being true is tied to how it is different -- none of this would have helped me at all when I was younger but I'm starting to realize why it is true now.

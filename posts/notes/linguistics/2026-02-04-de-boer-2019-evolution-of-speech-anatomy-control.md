. Linguists are not so much interested in all cognitive (and anatomical and physiological) machinery that is necessary for language but are mostly interested in the machinery that appears to be unique to humans.

traits that are derived from a common ancestor are called evolutionary homologues

similar traits not from common ancestors: evolutionary analogues

- Dunbar’s classic observation that the complexity of the repertoire of vocalizations correlates with group size for different monkey species

- larynx motor cortex has shown a continuous change between monkeys and apes, and this change has continued from apes to humans. This indicates that the changes cannot be solely attributed to selective pressure related to language but have to be attributed to other reasons (social vocalizations, for instance).
- Lower larynx and rounder round shape leads to more articulatory flexibility and a larger range of speech sounds

- From this, they conclude that breathing control and, hence, complex vocalizations must have evolved before the last common ancestor with Neanderthals. This is in line with the evidence gleaned from air sacs..

- Function of human chin, role in speech?
- Larynx evolved to close off the airway (1949), is now essential or producing speech.
- Cosmides and Tooby: certain cognitive tasks are much easier for human participants when presented as a social problem (i.e., cheater detection) than as an abstract puzzle.

- The adaptations consisted of finetuning of the anatomy of the vocal tract, but it appears that the major, and probably the earlier, adaptations must have had something to do with vocal (motor) control: control over breathing, control over the vocal folds, and the ability to imitate complex vocalizations and to analyze these in terms of a small set of building blocks. It is important to stress that all these abilities would be extensions and improvements of abilities that are present in other primates (monkeys and apes) and that were therefore most likely present in the last common ancestor of humans and other apes

One question I have after reading De Boer is on whether the comparative phonetic analysis of monkey and primate calls in the wild with humans is possible. Because other primates have a more complicated vocal tract than humans, it seems likely they would rely on a reduced set of sounds which are clearly expressible and reproducible. I also wonder if they exhibit similar capabilities for making distinctions between sounds as humans. Most individual humans have learned phonemic systems, which makes it difficult for them to pick up very fine-grained distinctions within speech.

Pragmatics requires reasoning about the minds of others.
A speaker has to think about
- the knowledge shared with the listener. 
A listener must make inferences about the speaker...
- inferences based on the context
- their knowledge about the speaker
- knowledge of general conventions of language use

Pragmatic capabilities that are central to language use are partially shaped by social learning. 

##### Competence is defined by...
- Access to a vocabulary and grammar
- When to deploy linguistic tools to achieve communicative goals

Deploying communication signals flexibly (based on context) is not unique to human communication. Captive chimpanzees use modality flexibly based on orientation and attentional state of audience.

**Claim:** Pragmatics in language involves cognitive capacity for *adapting signal use* based on knowledge or inferences of the mental states of others. "Ella got the car to stop" -> a manner implicature. 

In general, an infinite set of meanings can be associated with any given utterance in human language: linguistic *underdeterminancy*. Any known patterns are from a cultural perspective.

*Natural codes*: Signaler has associations between states of the world and signals, receiver has associations between signals and responses. 

Language is not a natural code.

- A natural code is based on associations between signals and relevant phenomena in the world. A conventional code (like language) on the other hand, is made possible by associations between signals and inferred speaker meanings.

Sperber and Wilson (1995):
- Informative intention: to inform the audience of something.
- Communicative intention: to inform the audience of one's informative intention. (Optional, e.g. "stop tickling me!", "look, an eagle!")

*Ostensive-inferential communication*
- tilt of the head, roll of the eyes:
	- Move someone's phone into line of sight. 
	
	> An act of ostension makes a receiver look for an informative intention—even if they do not directly see what the content of the informative intention is, recognizing that there is a communicative intention will motivate them to spend cognitive resources on inferring it
	
A second-order intentional system, then, is a system that also has beliefs and desires about the beliefs and desires of others

> These two studies provide the strongest evidence to date that non-human primates have something that looks like informative intentions in their natural communication. Informative intentions are, of course, only part of what it means to do ostensive-inferential communication, and the presence of informative intentions do not imply the presence of communicative intentions (see e.g., Bar-On, 2013).

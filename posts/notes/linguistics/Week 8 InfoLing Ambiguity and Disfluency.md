### Effect of Ambiguity and Lexical Availability on Syntactic and Lexical Production

Speakers did not consistently include words to circumvent temporary ambiguity, but did omit *that* when subsequent material was repeated. That is speakers choose syntactic structures to permit early mention of focus entities, not to circumvent temporary ambiguity.

The system that creates linguistic expressions must efficiently produce well-formed expressions, but also be easily comprehended. They test how word mention is influenced 1. by production efficiency (omission), and 2. by production to understanding (clarification with *that*)

- syntactic flexibility communicates subtle nuances in meaning --> active, passive constructions are different: *Mistakes were made*, *Mistakes that were made*. Thompson & Mulac argue *that* omission weakens distinction between main and embedded clauses, so high *epistemicity* or *topicality* of the complement lead to *that* being dropped.

Does *that* affect relative clause formation in CTPs?
- I can walk the dog.
- I made him walk (1SG) the dog. 
- I persuaded him (that) he walked the dog (SoA -> SoA)
- I persuaded him he walked the dog (SoA, no previous context)
- I thought (that) he walked the dog. -- past tense (SoA -> SoA)
- I thought he walked the dog -- past tense 
- I thought he walk the dog.

lemma: representation of syntactic properties of to-be-produced words. They encode the information necessary to construct syntactic structure, supports the notion of incremental production. 

==assumption of availability-based effect: production proceeds efficiently if syntactic structures are used which permit quickly selected lemmas to be mentioned as soon as possible.==

*The coach knew (that) you missed practice*, assume speaker has lemma for coach and know. ==If an embedded subject is selected quickly, *that*-less sentence complement accommodates the immediate mention of embedded subject== --> Availability-based effects

But also, ambiguity-sensitive sentence production. 
Garden paths occur when 1. sentences contain temporary syntactic ambiguity 2. are biased towards a syntactic analysis that is inappropriate.

*The coach knew (you ...)*
*The coach knew you ...*
Avoided by *The coach knew that (you ... )*

*The astronauts selected their ...*
*(The astronauts selected for the Apollo...) made history*
Avoided by *The astronauts who were selected ...*
But, no compelling evidence of full forms for garden path sentences.

Memory of syntactic structure of sentences is poor relative to meaning.
Sentence is stored in conceptual format, individual lexical items responsible for syntactic structure may remain activated, but ==not necessarily linked to conceptual content of the sentence==. Then sentence production takes over, entities and actions mapped to content words, relations mapped to grammar. 

We can verify this task model, by looking at how much inclusion/omission of optional function words is det. by memory of encoded sentence rather than other production factors. *That* is primarily syntactic structure:: if speakers regenerate by meaning *that* inclusion is unmotivated. They find memory accuracy low, suggests optional word mention sensitive to production --> availability, ambiguity-avoidance.

#### experiment 4,5,6
Investigate optional word mention guided by availability-based factors. Both main and embedded subject are I or you:

I knew (that) I had booked a flight for tomorrow (subj more available, nonamb.)
You knew (that) I had booked a flight for tomorrow (subj less availabe, nonamb.)
I knew (that) you had booked a flight for tomorrow (subj less available, ambg.)
You knew (that) you had booked a flight for tomorrow (subj more available, ambg.)

Hypothesis: *that* omission on 1 over 2, 4 over 3 because availability.
This was confirmed: speakers omit *that* in repetition of subject --> availability affects optional word mention. However, repetition manipulation may affect that mention for reasons other than increased availability. For instance, *coreference* could be responsible for observed effects.

Experiment 5 changes recall cue to post-that material (increasing availability), which should result in more *that* omission due to semantic integration. Both experiments provide evidence supporting availability of later material affects optional *that*.

6 was repetition of 4, investigating if communicative goals might increase availability over ambiguity avoidance.

- As a general principle, speakers do not mention optional words to disambiguate sentences (although they may in certain cases). The use of optional words is sensitive to the availability of the material that is spoken. 

These availability-based effects on order of mention (like the choice of a transitive structure) are consistent with a *fully lexical* model of sentence production.
- lexical items may influence wording decisions through syntactic mechanisms
- The paper suggests that production is not fully lexically based, but rather the structure choices that speakers make when influenced by availability result from the interplay between lexical activation and syntactic production mechanisms. They are definitely not fully syntactically based.

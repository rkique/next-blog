title: 'An orphanage'
---
An orphanage lies among the ruins of solemn-faced storefronts, far in the the desert. Nobody finds it on their own, everyone comes from somewhere inside. They are taught the basic methods of scavenging by those who came before them. The sun is always very hot.  The children go out in the mornings, before the heat warms the ruined asphalt. They look for wreckage, aircraft mostly, small vessels and flyers that have been grounded forever. They find tasty snacks with dog-earred wrappings, blankets, bottles, bags. Japanese tools for trimming and scoring and marking, paper mostly, sometimes skin. Sometimes they find dark and damp spaces where things grow. They do not know to grow things. They know how long the walk is and the last time they ate.

They get their water from a large still underground reservoir. They climb down drains, covers like doors, descend eight iron rungs, three flights of concrete stairs, navigating by touch and instinct. If one brought a light down, clasped between their teeth, they would see a perfect figure eight, almost two rings, lined with concrete. It is a place meant for thousands; but it is also not meant to be used at all. At the very least not in the way they do, cupping hands and sipping straight from the surface.

They are artistically inclined. They find pieces of detritus and bind them with rope and glue to the rooftops of their dwellings. What do they make? They have little use for maps. They are fascinated by animals, birds and lizards and mice. They add to the vertebrae of birds metal wings, put long plastic streamers under dead frogs.

They are interested in fashion. They sort through bins and racks between half-walls and shaded alcoves. Clothing for every possible purpose, each one speaking to a series of future events. Parkas, ski coats, cargo pants. Summer outfits, dresses, swimwear, cocktail attire. Slips and shirts with proclamations of being fixed in time and space. Identities created by an eliminatory process which mean nothing to them now, because there is nothing to take away.

They ignore the conversations in lights during the nighttime from the stars. They ignore the flashing, pleading papers found frequently among the remains of the dead.

The worst time was when they found a plane that crashed behind the grass yards. The lights extinguishing in the night like an ember . But the pilot was still breathing, the emergency beacon tapping out a steady red-black signal to the sky. That pilot woke to find them searching through the scattered packages in the back.

title: 'Sound and the Fury review'
---

Signet classic -- the appendix was prior to the book and added a lot of detail that I wouldn't have guessed otherwise. The part that I remember the most is how insufferable Jason IV is; he alone has a socially constructed identity. He ends up happy, though. The recollection of personal responsibility throughout the novel is brutal and all-consuming. 

There isn't a ton of interfamilial dynamics outside of Quentin and Caddy: two of the three perspectives taken are from outsiders, Benjy and Quentin. I wish that there was more time spent on the father figure, Jason III: the appendix paints a sympathetic picture of him. 

To understand the Compson's decline better I would have to have a more idealized image of a great Southern family. It seems like Faulkner takes for granted that readers intuit how great life was for them before they stopped owning plantations.

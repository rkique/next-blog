Typography can have potent connotations. Signs and material vehicles are embedded in a spatiotemporal context upon which the determination of their meaning may depend. 

Locke: "ideas" take the form of "mental images". 
- the medium contributes to the message and its particular affordances may even modify our intentions. 
- it is clearly not sustainable to regard a perceptible (‘sensible’) form as a necessary feature of all signs: mental entities are not apprehended by the senses.
- An inclusive definition of signs in general should thus refer more broadly to the material (rather than necessarily perceptible) form of the sign vehicle.

In traditional definitions, a sign is a sign *of* , or *for*, something else. While this is challenged by theories of language which look at its ability to signify, it reflects the function of language in use, which depends on signs being *taken as* signs of something. 

arbitrariness: signs that are made to be signs (especially words) can, in principle, symbolize anything at all

Although we are often cautioned that the sign is not the (represented) thing, such self‑referential examples may be taken to suggest a partial or metaphorical identity of sign vehicle and referent.

Referential signs are often reductively referred to as "substituting" for referents:

A perceptible or sensible form (a signifier), and the intelligible or rational content (signified, or concept). What is signified has two semantic dimensions, of reference and significance. Sense: conceptual meaning. Reference: referential meaning. 

*Indirect relationship between the sign vehicle and the referent*. 

A sign is classified as natural and "indicative" if its vehicle has an inherent connection to its referent (e.g. a shadow, or a symptom of disease). indicative signs involve *no communicative intent.*

Contiguity, similarity. Not only something being physically a part of something else, but also being in the same semantic domain as something else. 

There are VERY commonplace rules which are learned about non-linguistic signs. Semioticians are fond of mentioning the traffic light system as a non‑linguistic example of this. The meaning of traffic signs is based on convention, and therefore has to be learned. 

Saussure makes it very clear that he is not dealing with the dimension of *reference*: 
==‘The linguistic sign unites, not a thing and a name, but a concept and an acoustic image"==

Saussure notes that it is not the metal in a coin that fixes its value. 

The linguistic sign system.. is reflected as a cognitive system in the minds of individuals but it exists in its entirety only in the masse parlante— the community of speakers.

Meaning is always the *social* product of the language system. 

If we wish to understand Saussure’s conception of the language system (as distinct from its use) in accord with his own intentions, we must attempt to exclude the concept of reference. E.g. all signs refer only to their sensible counterparts and not to any real lived experiences which may be seen as relevant (to native speakers of a language, none of its classifications appear arbitrary). 

Saussure’s radical stance is that the language system does not need to refer to extralingual reality. What of computer languages and game making? What of making games which take on no natural reference except those which others associate with them?

Peirce proposes that limits to interpretation derive from both nature and culture. It is in the sense of deepening understanding that ‘a sign is something by knowing which we know something more’.  The object constrains or 'grounds' the meaning and 'an endless series of representations ... may be considered to have an absolute object as its limit'. 

Francis bacon: 'The subtlety of nature is greater many times over than the subtlety of argument' (The material world constrains interpretation). 

Peirce argues that ‘all thinking is dialogic in form. Your self of one instant appeals to your deeper self for his assent’. 

Peirce sign relations:

1 Symbolic: based on a relationship that is fundamentally unmotivated, arbitrary, or purely conventional—so that it must be agreed upon and learned, e.g. language in general (plus specific languages, alphabetical letters, punctuation marks, words, phrases, and sentences), numbers, Morse code, traffic lights, national flags.

2 Iconic: based on perceived resemblance or imitation (involving some recognizably similar property or attribute, such as appearance, sound, feeling, taste, or smell), e.g. a portrait, a cartoon, onomatopoeia, metaphors, realistic sounds in ‘programme music’, sound effects in radio drama, a dubbed film soundtrack, imitative gestures. 

3 Indexical: based on direct connection (physical or causal). This link can be observed or inferred, e.g. ‘natural signs’ (smoke, thunder, footprints, echoes, non‑synthetic odours and flavours), 
medical symptoms (pain, a rash, pulse‑rate), measuring instruments (weathercock, thermometer, clock, spirit‑level), 
‘signals’ (a knock on a door, a phone ringing)
pointers (a pointing ‘index’ finger, a directional signpost)
recordings (a photograph, a film, video, or television shot, an audio‑recorded voice), person

Pierce has three fundamental ontological categories for phenomena known as firstness, secondness, and thirdness,

Firstness is the inherent quality of the sign. 
- qualisign: icon, color sample. 
- sinsign: icon or index. indexical signs. 
- legisign: symbol. words and traffic lights.

Secondness is the domain of 'brutal fact'. The hereness and ndowness of them'. These qualities given in perception.
- these are the sign relations
	- iconic
	- indexical
	- symbolic

Thirdness, the process of mediation, involved in representation and communication. Domain of 'law', habit, and relatedness. The thirdness brings the firstness and secondness into relation.

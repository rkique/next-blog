Aristotle observed that if X is a performance verb ("build), then "A is (now) Xing" entails "A has not (yet) Xed"; however, if X is an activity verb ("live"), then "A is (now) Xing" entails "A has Xed". This is the difference of accomplishment and activity.

- This is relevant to Cristofaro's study of subordination where activity verbs are classified as having a higher semantic integration (corresponding SoA) than performance verbs.

Is "John recites a poem" really more frequentative than singular? "Appear in a narrative in the historical present" feels like "telling a story", and I think that's the most common use of this sentence.

In activity verbs, it is harder to have the secondary interpretation of accomplishments e.g. "John almost painted a picture" could have John paint most of a picture while "John almost walked" does not.

most activity verbs have accomplishment senses in special contexts ("look" -> "looking at the paper you sent me")

A bigger challenge

accomplishment verbs which take direct objects unexpectedly behave like activities IF an indefinite plural or mass-noun is substituted for the definite one:

(59)
John ate the bag of popcorn in an hour <-> *John ate popcorn in an hour.

*John discovered the buried treasure in his backyard for six weeks. <-> John discovered fleas on his dog for 6 weeks. 

*John discovered that quaint little village for years. <-> Tourists discovered that quaint little village for years.

Sooo the boundary between achievement and activity verbs are blurred. Around 1967, we could have postulated syntactic features such as +-durative and state selectional restrictions, for example between verbs with these features and time adverbials like for x time and in x time. Verkuyl was very aware of principle (59): main thesis is that notions of durative and perfective aspect are not to be found in any one constituent in surface structure, but arise from the "composition" of certain constituents.

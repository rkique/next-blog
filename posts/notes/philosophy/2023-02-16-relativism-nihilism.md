Mackie's Internalist Argument for Nihilism

(1) Moral goodness would have to be such that sincere judgement about it is intrinsically motivational. 
(2) But there is no property such that sincere judgement about it is intrinsically motivational.
(3) So, there is no such thing as moral goodness.
On the whole, most people who judge something good generally are thereby motivated...

Humean premise (2): For the premise to be false, there would have to be a property whose apprehension would move any rational being as such. The apprehension itself would have already to be a motivational state. It is this internal connection that Humeans deny. They say: representation of the world as being this way or that, is one thing, and motivation is
another. For a representation of the way the world is to make a difference in our deciding what to do, it must first be joined with some sort of desire, or valuing, or aim of ours. 

Dreier's Internalist Argument for Relativism

Moral goodness is such that sincere judgment about it intrinsically motivates.

But, which properties motivate depends on the psychology of the judging agent.

So, which properties are the moral ones depends on the psychology of the agent.

If internalism is true, then morality is either unreal, or relative.

Suppose we have a terrorist who says: (A) The intentional slaughter of millions of innocent non-combatants is morally justified as a means to promote my end.

And we have anti-terrorist leaders say (B) The intentional slaughter of millions of innocent non-combatants cannot be morally justified under any circumstances.

Fairly obvious points: (B) disagrees with (A), and (B) could be prefaced by "(A) is not true". However, Relativism denies both these points. The relativist thinks of (A) and (B) as containing an implicit relativization to the speaker's moral outlook. There's never *really* a moral disagreement, hm?

One possible defense is that if a police officer states that he saw the defendant enter and leave one hour later, and the defense says "can you provide an inertial time frame?", we wouldn't say that his statement is incorrect. The incorrect *theory* of the Policeman's 'one hour' does not infect the integrity or veracity of his ordinary, first order judgment.

So long as the ordinary judgements are understood relative to whatever standards make most sense in the context, they can be perfectly correct just as the policeman's judgements are perfectly correct if understood relative to the inertial frame of the courtroom. Nihilism is no more called for in ethics than it is in common sense physics.

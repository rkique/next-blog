### Geach's perspective: it's about language

No. Moore is wrong, we don't need metaphysics, epistemology, or anything like that. Linguistic investigation shows that "GOOD" does not function like a property name!

See, adjective contribute a property, or an extension, to the content of a sentence.

> The sun is yellow.
> This is a red book.

<s, <e,t>>

Adjectives contribute meaning independently. You may "pull apart" "Elizabeth was a British philosopher" to say "Elizabeth was British" and "Elizabeth was a philosopher". 

At the level of the whole, sentences are the fundamental unit when assigning meaning to language. 

The predicativity test for properties

Property: American
"John is an american kicker".
"John is a husband". 
"John is an american husband". 

Property: Good 
"John is a good kicker".
"John is a husband".
"John is a good husband" (No!)

Geach: If good was a property, then it would pass the predicativity test.

We have *alienans*, where the subject is not the substantive: Quigley is a fake duck. That painting is a forged Picasso. Creation science. An argument can be made that "bad" is *alienans* where we cannot safely predicate of a bad A what we predicate of A.

Maybe Good is a gradable adjective. Gradable Adjectives are context sensitive. For example, "short" and "tall" are gradable because a short basketball player is usually not a short person.

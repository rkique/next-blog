## On Moral Properties

One analogy we can draw:

Moral properties are REALIZED by natural properties.
Moral properties are CONSTITUTED by natural properties.
Moral properties are COMPOSED by natural properties.
But they are not the natural -- ! --

Moral properties are like a chess game, realized by a bunch of pieces of wood, moved around. Moral properties don't cause anything, and you know them a priori. But they are not the pieces of wood.

### Paleo-non-naturalism: Moore
Everybody thinks they know what he meant. What did Moore mean by "non-natural properties"?

### New version
Shafer-Landau, Enoch. 
Moral facts are a priori known, causally isolated, and necessary.

hard naturalism: jackson, sturgeon: no need to make normative claims because the morals are all there in the natural. All moral properties can be reduced to natural definitions Wittgenstein's language games -- ! -- something along the lines of "we'll take our interesting questions and formulate them properly so that we can pass them along."

soft naturalism: though all facts are natural, for whatever reason we still need to make normative claims and that's okay. We might think of it being something like "the shape of the concepts don't match up very well"

"gricean maxims".
- you cannot refuse to use other people's language
  
**non-naturalism says**: There are some important irreducibly normative facts, which we could state only be making such irreducibly normative claims.

### PARFIT AGAINST SOFT NATURALISM
Parfit does not think that soft naturalism is possible. 
"D being what we ought to do" is "being an N act", where N is a non-normative description.

Either these two concepts (N) ("what we ought to do")

- refer to the same property (hard naturalism)
- does more than that
  - this further information in D is irreducibly normative (naturalism is false)
  - it isn't (soft naturalism is false, as we reduce N)

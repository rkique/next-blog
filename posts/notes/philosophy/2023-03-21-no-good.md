Essentially the point of Valvova's argument is that if we have "no good reason" to believe something then we have no good reason to believe anything, including believing that we have no good reason. Including our perceptual beliefs, like "there is night and day" or "trees exist"! There must be *some* chain of reasoning that is really valid; to say that none of it is valid because evolution is going too far. The Darwinian hypothesis against moral facts cuts too low on the tree of our belief/reason system for it to be truly effective.

possibility of error support for "no good" is akin to self-indication assumption (aka it's flawed!) After all of course if you select a random point in the universe you are not likely to find a human, and yet you naturally expect other humans around them.

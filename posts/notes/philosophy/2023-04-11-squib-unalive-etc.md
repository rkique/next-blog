### unalive

"Unalive" is an interesting new word. It is the opposite of "alive", in the sense that it implies that the agent was once alive. But it must be a verb. Most frequently, it refers to persons, and it means one of the following:

[BECOME $\lnot$ [ALIVE]] (die)

CAUSE [BECOME $\lnot$ [ALIVE]] (kill)

where as we have seen, 'become' is defined as an individual x, state S, and time i, and means that: S(i)(x) and not S(x)(i - 1)(x).

Critically, it cannot be used in any adjectival form, such as $\lnot$ [ALIVE], which is the conventional application of "dead". 

Example of second language english speaker:

> (dead) "tamten or tamto?? both are unalive things ... "

> (1SG die) "Has anyone gone to the ER wanting to unalive due to insomnia?

> (reflexive kill) Chemistry makes me want to unalive myself.

> (TR kill) curious about what type of fungus this is ... started growing on a tree where my aunt unalives chickens.

Unalive can be used to refer reflexively and unreflexively.

In conclusion, unalive differs from our expectations is that it is employed as a verb meaning "die" or "kill", and not the state "dead". ### very possible

Most adjectives exhibit monotonicity of degrees. For example, if someone is very tall then they are also tall. However, there is a class of adjectives which do not intuitively have this property, and I think the classic example here is possible.

It is not really true that "very possible" has the same meaning as possible.

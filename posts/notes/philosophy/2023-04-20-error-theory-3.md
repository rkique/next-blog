### Elimination, or revision?

Mackie and Street agree that there are no moral facts. Mackie believes that morality is a myth and there is no moral right or wrong. Street believes that it is in fine shape, even though it is simply invented by us. 

Maybe it's like this:

We thought that X's would have F and G.

But there is nothing like that.

But was being F and G built into the concept of X?

Yes: then we have discovered that there are no Xs. This is the elimination, the null hypothesis.
No: then we have discovered that the X's were not as we thought. This is revision, the revised hypothesis.

### NAIVE PHYSICS

Before learning about science, we usually think of a solid object as something that fills the space within its boundaries, continuous, and contains no empty spaces (unlike a sponge). 

But there are no such things.
We are not inclined to say that science has discovered that there are no solid objects. We would rather have a revised (scientific?) version of solidity.

Because we treat solids as a revisable concept! Fundamentally, solids are still intuitively "solid".

### NAIVE WITCHES

A witch is a woman who 
- is socially outcast
- seems odd, eccentric
- can do magic and communes with satan

But we have discovered that there are no such women. Why don't we say, like with solids, that "witches aren't magic or satanic after all"?

Because we treat witches as an eliminable concept! Fundamentally, witches would no longer be intuitively "witch-like".

### Categoricity as an Illocutionary force
Here is why categoricity is an illocutionary force:
You are allowed to ask: "yes, but what's that to me?"

You must take the A Train.
 - Why?
 - It's the fastest way to get to Sugar Hill.
 - *Oh, but I don't want to go to Sugar Hill*
 - Okay, never mind then.

You ought to treat your workers with more respect.
 - *Oh, but I don't care about them.*
 - Okay, never mind then... no, wait!

Another similarity is that its hard to imagine someone forcing a moral requirement in an unmoral way; edicts of etiquette are seldom mentioned with an unetiquette way. Maybe Chesterson's fence is at play here...

### Motivating Reasons

Original definition of what a "reason" is:
A's reason to F: A's desire to X + A's belief that Fing will X.

But we can have a desire and a belief without a reason.
The guy drinking a petrol cocktail *thinks* he has a reason. We *don't think* he has a reason.

But we have another way to formulate it (Mark Shroeder):
A's reason to F: A's belief if true, would explain why Fing will X.

Complaint:
A's reason to F: A's belief that giving you the injection will prevent the (low probability) disease.

Similarly to "volunteer" notion of moralism: Owen Wingrave has no internal motivation to join the army. Owen's father maintains that he has a reason to join the army. He means this in an external sense. What is that sense? Williams says: there are no external reasons.

Reasons: reasons are explainers. 
Reason to live vs. Reason to go vegetarian.
If something can be a reason for an action, then it could be someone's reason for acting on a particular occasion, and it would then figure in an explanation of that action.

To vindicate morality, requirements must derive from our reasons.
But, all of our TRUE reasons are internal.
The contents of a person's S are contingent and not necessarily optional.
So any substantive requirement of morality can have rational authority only for those in Foot's "volunteer army".

But wait a minute. Humeans doubt the power of practical reason to produce moral motivation. Humeans believe that two things can be true or false: empirical results, and knowledge about the relation of ideas. Reasoning can't produce motivation. Or, reasoning can't produce *moral* motivation.

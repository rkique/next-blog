## What exactly does the word unalive mean?

Unalive is a very interesting new word in online culture. Although it derives from the word "alive", it is not a noun but a verb. It shares some properties with "kill", and perhaps with "die", but which exactly?

In class, we noted that from a compositional viewpoint, the word *dead* does not mean *not alive*. Rather, it can only apply to individuals who have been alive, e.g.

t_-: e [[alive]]
t_0: e NOT [[alive]]

In this way, Abe Lincoln is dead, but not the Eiffel Tower. 
Here, we will also assume we have die and kill as composed of one another:

die: become [[dead]]
kill: cause [[become [[dead]]]]

Depending on the context, unalive can mean "die", "kill", or "kill myself". Here are representative examples for each (source: reddit)

- (die) "Has anyone gone to the ER wanting to unalive due to insomnia?"

- (kill myself) "I wish I had unalive pills right now".
  
- (kill) "... started growing on a tree where my aunt unalives chickens"

## unalive is a "complete" kill

My hypothesis is that "unalive" is a more complete version of the word "kill": it has the regular transitive usage, and the reflexive use in the intransitive.


A compositional definition for unalive must involve "alive", not in the sense of an opposite ("unemployed"), but in the sense of reversal, as with "undo", "unpack", or "unzip". Someone can be unemployed without ever having been employed, but you cannot undo something that has not been done. In the same way, you must be alive to unalive.

Yet the word "kill" lacks an intransitive version that means "to kill oneself". For the purposes of online communication, this use of kill appears much more frequently than its *true* intransitive, that is "they/he/she kill". However, "they/he/she unalived" has that exact meaning, that is "they/he/she killed herself". 


## slay

It's worth noting that roughly analogous to "kill", there is a popular term "slay" with positive connotations. For example, if a friend gives a presentation in class, it is common for someone to say that they "slayed" or just the exclamative/imperative "slay!". This can probably be traced to the older expression "they/he/she killed it". One possible rationale for the informal "slay" replacing the informal "kill" in the intransitive is that the conventional use of intransitive "slay" is slightly more appropriate than the intransitive form of "kill". Compare:

"Borcs coming left and right all night, they slayed till dawn".
"Borcs coming left and right all night, they killed till dawn".

Let's go back to "unalive". I contend that "unalive" means "kill", but with the same structure as transitive-intransitive words like "wash". In the base case, it can be thought of as an transitive verb with a subject and object. In the intransitive case, null complement anaphora fills in the object. 

"I wash him"
"I wash" (implies "I wash myself")

"I unalive him"
"I unalive" (implies "I unalive myself")

Importantly, "kill" does not have this usage:
"I kill him"
"I kill" 

The most common form we see unalive is "I want to unalive" or "I want to unalive myself". Although in traditional usage we might find "I want to die" and "I want to kill myself" as parallel, it is much more useful to compare against "I want to wash" and "I want to wash myself". 

It is clear they have equivalent meaning. That is, "I want to unalive" does not mean "I want to die". It means "I want to kill" in the reflexive intransitive, or "I want to kill myself". It is an economical way to say "kill myself", that avoids both the negative connotations of "commit suicide" and the passivity of "die".

Moore: *Good* is a simple, non-natural property. 
The Open Question Argument (Then is N good? is a reasonable question for all N) shows that good must be "simple". But it is non-natural. Our reasoning might be as follows. Can we imagine "good" as existing by itself in time, and not merely as a property of some natural object? Moore believes that we cannot. For the majority of properties of objects (roundess, redness, squareness) can be imagined independent of the existence of those objects. However, good cannot be; and so it is non-natural property.

These characteristics Moore considers here as natural seem much more like non-natural characteristics, not natural ones. Brownness is certainly not the collection of brown objects (although from a linguistic perspective it might be treated as such).

Moore cannot articulate why he believes good is non-natural. He insists in Principia Ethica that two and four do not *exist* in the saem way that natural things do, but that "two and two are four" means something. This is because he built his lectures around the idea that "being" and "existence" were two separate conditions, which he later retracted.
